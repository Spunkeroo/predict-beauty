What Clean Beauty Actually Means

Clean beauty lacks a universal legal definition, which creates confusion. In practice, clean beauty products avoid ingredients with strong evidence of harm: parabens, formaldehyde donors, synthetic fragrance blends, and certain phthalates. However, 'clean' does not automatically mean 'better' or 'safer.' Water and sunlight are natural but can damage skin. The best approach is evidence-based beauty: choosing products with proven safe AND effective ingredients.

Ingredients to Actually Avoid (Evidence-Based)

Based on current scientific consensus, avoid: formaldehyde-releasing preservatives (DMDM hydantoin, quaternium-15), high concentrations of certain essential oils on sensitive skin, talc from unverified sources, and coal tar dyes. Many commonly feared ingredients like parabens (in cosmetic concentrations) and silicones have strong safety profiles according to independent toxicology reviews.

The Science Behind Natural Ingredients

Vitamin C from kakadu plum is more stable than synthetic ascorbic acid. Bakuchiol provides retinol-like benefits without irritation (clinically validated). Centella asiatica (cica) has centuries of use and modern studies confirming wound healing and anti-inflammatory properties. Squalane (olive-derived) is biomimetic to human sebum for superior moisturization.
